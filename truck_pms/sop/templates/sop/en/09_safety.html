Chapter 24: General Workshop Safety & PPE

24.1 The Safety Culture Principle

Safety is not a set of rules — it is a mindset. Every mechanic, driver, and supervisor in this organization is expected to:

  • Stop any task that feels unsafe, without fear of reprisal.
  • Speak up when they see an unsafe act or condition involving anyone.
  • Lead by example — wear proper PPE even for "quick" tasks.
  • Report every near-miss — a near-miss today prevents an accident tomorrow.
⚠ Golden Rule: If you cannot do a job safely, do not do it at all. No job is so urgent that it cannot be done safely.

24.2 General Workshop Safety Rules

The following rules apply at all times within the workshop, depot, or any location where truck maintenance is performed:

  1. Housekeeping: Keep all work areas clean and organized. Clean up spills immediately. Return tools to their designated storage after use. Do not leave tools, parts, or debris on the floor — they are tripping hazards. Dispose of rags contaminated with oil, fuel, or solvent in approved fire-safe containers.
  2. Spill Containment: Any spill of oil, fuel, coolant, or other fluids must be contained immediately using absorbent pads or granular absorbent. Report spills over 5 liters to the supervisor. Do not wash spills down drains — use proper disposal procedures.
  3. Ventilation: Ensure adequate ventilation when working with solvents, paints, welding equipment, or running engines indoors. Carbon monoxide from running engines is lethal in enclosed spaces. Use exhaust extraction hoses for all running engines.
  4. Fire Prevention: No smoking in the workshop. Store flammable liquids (fuel, solvents, paints) in approved fire-rated cabinets. Keep ignition sources away from battery charging areas (hydrogen gas). Know the location of all fire extinguishers and emergency exits.
  5. Compressed Air Safety: Never use compressed air to clean clothing, skin, or hair — air can enter the bloodstream through small cuts and cause fatal embolisms. Never point an air nozzle at another person. Use pressure regulators — do not exceed rated tool pressure.
  6. Confined Space: Tank compartments, pits, and storage tanks are confined spaces. NEVER enter a confined space without: a confined space permit, atmospheric testing (oxygen, LEL, toxic gas), continuous ventilation, a safety harness and retrieval line, and a trained attendant outside the space.
  7. Hot Work: Welding, grinding, cutting, or any activity that produces sparks requires a hot work permit. Clear the area of flammable materials within 10 meters. Have a fire watcher with an extinguisher present. Monitor for 30 minutes after work is completed.
  8. Drugs and Alcohol: Working under the influence of drugs or alcohol is strictly prohibited. Prescription medications that cause drowsiness must be reported to the supervisor before starting work.
  9. Personal Conduct: Horseplay, running, and practical jokes in the workshop are prohibited. Use designated walkways. Do not jump off loading docks or truck beds.
  10. Reporting: All injuries, no matter how minor, must be reported to the supervisor and recorded in the incident log. All near-misses must be reported and investigated.

24.5 Proper Tool Handling & Safety

24.5.1 Hand Tools — General Rules

  1. Use the right tool for the job. Never use a wrench as a hammer, a screwdriver as a pry bar, or pliers as a wrench. Using the wrong tool damages both the tool and the fastener, and increases injury risk.
  2. Inspect before use. Check wrenches for cracked jaws, sockets for worn drive ends, screwdrivers for mushroomed tips, hammers for loose heads. Any damaged tool must be removed from service and tagged for repair or disposal.
  3. Pull — do not push. Whenever possible, pull a wrench toward your body rather than pushing away. If you must push, use the palm of your hand with an open hand (not a clenched fist) to avoid smashed fingers if the wrench slips.
  4. Never extend a wrench with a pipe or cheater bar unless the wrench is specifically rated for such use. Using a cheater bar on a standard wrench can cause the jaw to snap catastrophically.
  5. Keep cutting tools sharp. Dull tools require more force and are more likely to slip. Store sharp tools with blades covered or in a designated drawer.
  6. Do not carry tools in pockets. Use a tool belt or tool cart. Sharp or pointed tools in pockets can cause injury when bending or sitting.

24.5.4 Vehicle Support Safety (Jacks & Stands)

⚠ CRITICAL — LIFTING SAFETY: The single most deadly mistake in truck maintenance is working under a vehicle supported only by a hydraulic jack. A jack can fail suddenly due to seal blowout, hydraulic leak, or mechanical failure. ALWAYS use properly rated jack stands. Never use cinder blocks, concrete blocks, or stacked lumber as vehicle supports — they can crumble without warning.

Proper Lifting Procedure:

  1. Park on solid, level ground. Engage parking brake. Chock both sides of the wheels that remain on the ground.
  2. Identify correct lifting points on the frame rails or axle (refer to manufacturer's manual). Never lift on the differential, steering components, or body panels.
  3. Position the jack saddle with a block of wood (to prevent metal-on-metal contact and slipping).
  4. Lift the vehicle slowly and steadily. Watch for any shifting or instability.
  5. Once at desired height, place jack stands under the frame at the designated lift points. Use stands of the same type, in pairs, at the same height setting.
  6. Adjust the ratchet or pin mechanism — ensure it is fully engaged and cannot collapse.
  7. Gently lower the vehicle onto the stands. Do not lower with a bang. Lower until the full weight is on the stands and the jack is free.
  8. Test the stability by pushing the vehicle laterally at shoulder height. If it rocks, adjust stands or reposition.
  9. Only then may you work under the vehicle. The jack may be left in place as a secondary safety measure but must NOT bear the main load.

Jack Stand Ratings: Use stands rated for at least 1.5x the weight on that end of the vehicle. For a typical loaded tanker weighing 20,000 kg on the rear tandem: use stands rated at 30,000 lbs (13,600 kg) per pair minimum — two stands per axle.

24.6.2 Safe Chemical Handling Rules

  1. Read the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) before using any chemical you are unfamiliar with. SDS must be available in the workshop (physical binder or digital access).
  2. Use the correct chemical for the job. Do not substitute — using the wrong solvent can damage components, create toxic fumes, or cause a fire.
  3. Never mix chemicals. Mixing bleach with ammonia produces deadly chlorine gas. Mixing acids with solvents can cause explosions. If in doubt, do not mix.
  4. Use proper dispensing equipment. Use pumps, funnels, or spouts — never pour from large containers without control. Use a bonding wire when transferring flammable liquids between metal containers (prevents static spark).
  5. Keep containers closed when not in use. Open containers evaporate, creating fire hazards and exposing workers to vapors.
  6. Waste disposal: Used oil, coolant, solvents, and oily rags are hazardous waste. Dispose of each in designated, labeled containers. Never pour chemicals down drains, onto the ground, or into trash bins.
  7. Spill response: Know the location of the spill kit. For chemical spills: contain with absorbent pads or booms. For fuel spills: eliminate all ignition sources. For acid spills: neutralize with baking soda before absorbing. Report spills to supervisor — documentation may be required for regulatory compliance.

24.7 Lockout / Tagout (LOTO) Procedure

Lockout/Tagout is used to ensure equipment is isolated from all energy sources before maintenance begins. The following energy sources exist in the workshop and on trucks:

  • Electrical: Battery power (12/24V), shore power, charger connections
  • Pneumatic: Compressed air in brake system, shop air lines
  • Hydraulic: Power steering, hydraulic lift gates, water brake systems
  • Mechanical (stored energy): Spring brake chambers, suspension springs, pressurized tanks
  • Thermal: Hot engine, exhaust, cooling system under pressure
  • Chemical: Fuel in tanks/lines, battery acid, refrigerants

Six-Step LOTO Procedure:

  1. NOTIFY all affected personnel that equipment will be locked out and why.
  2. SHUT DOWN the equipment using normal shutdown procedures (turn off engine, etc.).
  3. ISOLATE all energy sources: disconnect battery (negative first), close fuel valves, drain air pressure, release spring brakes, etc.
  4. LOCK & TAG each isolation point with your personal padlock and a tag stating: your name, date, reason, and expected return time. Tags must be weather-resistant and attached with a non-reusable zip tie or wire.
  5. VERIFY zero energy: attempt to start the engine (it should not start), test for voltage with a multimeter, verify air pressure is zero, manually release stored energy (e.g., pry against a spring with caution), check that the cooling system is depressurized.
  6. PROCEED with work. Only the person who applied the lock may remove it. To remove: complete the work, ensure all tools are cleared, ensure all guards are replaced, notify affected personnel, remove locks and tags.
⚠ Exception — "Hot Work" LOTO: Welding or grinding on a fuel tanker requires additional steps: purge the tank compartment (gas-free certificate), open all manlids for ventilation, continuously monitor with a combustible gas detector (LEL), have a fire watcher present with extinguisher, and secure a hot work permit from the supervisor.

Workshop Recommendation: All workshop extinguishers should be ABC-rated multi-purpose dry chemical (Type ABC, 5-10 lb capacity). One extinguisher per 200 square meters, with a travel distance of no more than 15 meters to the nearest extinguisher. Check monthly (gauge in green, tamper seal intact).

24.8.2 PASS Extinguisher Operation

  1. Pull the pin — break the tamper seal.
  2. Aim at the base of the fire — not at the flames.
  3. Squeeze the handle — slowly and evenly.
  4. Sweep side to side — covering the entire base of the fire.

If the fire does not extinguish after one extinguisher, evacuate immediately and call emergency services (local fire department). Do not attempt to fight a fire that is spreading rapidly, involves gas cylinders, or is located in a confined space.

24.8.3 Fire Prevention in the Workshop

  • No smoking anywhere inside the workshop or within 10 meters of fuel storage areas.
  • Store flammable liquids in approved flammable storage cabinets. Cabinet doors must be self-closing and latched.
  • Oily rags: store in metal containers with self-closing lids. Empty daily.
  • Charging batteries: charge in a designated area with ventilation (hydrogen gas is explosive). Do not charge near flammables.
  • Electrical panels: keep clear — at least 1 meter of unobstructed space. Do not store anything against electrical panels.
  • No extension cords as permanent wiring. Use power bars with built-in circuit breakers.
  • Hot work (welding, grinding, cutting): clear combustibles, have extinguisher ready, post a fire watch for 30 minutes after work completes.
  • Fuel transfer: bond and ground containers. No smoking. Engine off. Have absorbent pads and a fire extinguisher positioned nearby.

24.9 Safe Lifting & Ergonomics

24.9.1 Manual Lifting — Proper Technique

  1. Size up the load: Before lifting, push or tilt the object to gauge its weight. If it is too heavy (over 23 kg / 50 lb for one person), get mechanical assistance (forklift, hoist, or second person).
  2. Stance: Feet shoulder-width apart, one foot slightly ahead of the other for stability.
  3. Bend your knees — not your back. Keep the natural curve of your spine (do not round your back).
  4. Grip firmly: Use full palm grip, not just fingertips. Wear gloves if the object has sharp edges.
  5. Lift with your legs: Straighten your legs while keeping your back straight. Keep the load close to your body.
  6. Do not twist: Turn by moving your feet, not by rotating your torso while carrying the load.
  7. Set down: Reverse the process — bend your knees, keep back straight, lower slowly.

25.2 Fire Emergency

  1. SHOUT "FIRE! FIRE! FIRE!" to alert everyone in the area.
  2. EVACUATE the immediate area. Close doors behind you if safe to do so (contains the fire).
  3. ACTIVATE the fire alarm if the workshop has one.
  4. ATTEMPT TO EXTINGUISH only if ALL of the following apply:
    • The fire is small (trash can size or smaller).
    • You have a clear escape route behind you.
    • You have been trained on extinguisher use (PASS method).
    • You are using the correct extinguisher for the fire class.
    • No gas cylinders, fuel tanks, or hazardous materials are involved.
  5. If fire grows, or if you cannot extinguish it: evacuate, call the fire department, and account for all personnel at the designated assembly point.
  6. DO NOT re-enter the building for any reason until fire officials declare it safe.

25.3 Fuel / Chemical Spill Emergency

  1. ASSESS the spill: is it a small drip (can be absorbed with a rag) or a major release (flowing liquid, large pool)?
  2. Small spill: absorb with rags or absorbent pads. Place absorbed material in a hazardous waste container. Clean area with degreaser.
  3. Major spill:
    • Protect drains: Place absorbent booms or drain covers around drains immediately.
    • Eliminate ignition sources: No smoking, no electrical equipment operation, turn off any running engines in the area.
    • Contain: Use absorbent booms to surround the spill and prevent spreading. Use granular absorbent to soak up liquid within the containment area.
    • Notify: Call the supervisor and safety officer immediately. For fuel spills over 25 liters, regulatory reporting may be required.
    • Clean up: Collect all contaminated absorbent materials into hazardous waste drums. Label and seal drums for proper disposal.
    • Document: Complete a spill report with: date, time, location, product spilled, estimated volume, cause, and corrective actions taken.

25.4 Injury / Medical Emergency

  1. Call for help: Shout for assistance or call emergency services. Do not move the injured person unless they are in immediate danger (fire, chemical exposure, collapsing structure).
  2. First Aid — life-threatening conditions (ABC):
    • A - Airway: Check if the person is conscious. Tilt head back, lift chin to open airway.
    • B - Breathing: Look, listen, and feel for breathing for 10 seconds. If not breathing normally, begin CPR (30 chest compressions at 100-120/min, then 2 rescue breaths).
    • C - Circulation: Control severe bleeding with direct pressure using a clean cloth or bandage. Raise the injured area above the heart if possible. Apply a tourniquet only for life-threatening limb bleeding that cannot be controlled with direct pressure.
  3. Chemical exposure:
    • Eyes: Flush with clean water from the eyewash station for at least 15 minutes. Hold eyelids open. Remove contact lenses if present.
    • Skin: Remove contaminated clothing. Rinse affected area with water for at least 15 minutes. Use soap and water for non-corrosive chemicals.
    • Inhalation: Move the person to fresh air immediately. If not breathing, begin CPR.
    • Ingestion: Call Poison Control. Do NOT induce vomiting unless instructed by Poison Control. Do not give anything by mouth to an unconscious person.
  4. Report: Every injury, no matter how minor, must be reported to the supervisor and recorded in the company's incident log. This is not optional — even a small cut can become infected, and reporting protects both the worker and the company.

25.6 Incident Reporting & Investigation

All incidents (injuries, near-misses, property damage, environmental spills) must be reported within 2 hours to the supervisor, and formally documented within 24 hours.

Incident Report Form — Minimum Required Information:

  1. Date, time, and exact location of the incident.
  2. Names of all persons involved and witnesses.
  3. Description of what happened (who, what, when, where, how).
  4. Equipment involved (truck number, tools, chemicals used).
  5. Injuries sustained (nature and severity).
  6. First aid or medical treatment provided.
  7. Root cause (what conditions or actions led to this incident?).
  8. Corrective actions (what will be done to prevent recurrence?).
  9. Signature of person reporting and supervisor.
No-Blame Culture: The purpose of incident reporting is prevention, not punishment. Workers who report incidents honestly will not face disciplinary action. Retaliation against anyone who reports a safety concern is a serious violation of company policy.

Chapter 26: HAZMAT & Dangerous Goods Compliance

26.1 Applicable Regulations

All petroleum transportation activities are subject to national and international dangerous goods regulations. The operator and driver are jointly responsible for compliance. Non-compliance can result in fines, vehicle impoundment, and denial of depot access.

Key regulations for petroleum tanker operations:

  • Dangerous Goods Regulations (DGR): National implementation of UN Model Regulations for transport of dangerous goods by road.
  • Depot Safety Rules: Each petroleum depot has its own safety requirements — these may vary and must be reviewed.
  • Environmental Regulations: Governing spill prevention, discharge reporting, and waste disposal.

26.3 Placarding Requirements

Each truck transporting dangerous goods must display placards on all four sides (front, rear, both sides):

  • Hazard Class Placard: Class 3 (Flammable Liquid) — red diamond with flame symbol. Minimum size: 250 mm × 250 mm.
  • UN Number Placard: Orange panel (or integrated into hazard placard) showing the appropriate UN number for the product being carried.
  • Multi-compartment trucks: If carrying different products in different compartments, placard for the highest hazard product. Some jurisdictions require separate placarding per side if different products are loaded.
  • Empty (not gas-free) tanks: Must still display the placard for the last product carried. Only gas-free and certified empty tanks may remove placards.

26.4 Transport Documentation

Each trip must carry the following documents in the cab, accessible to the driver and emergency responders:

  1. Dangerous Goods Transport Document (DG Document / Waybill): Including: shipper name and address, consignee name and address, description of goods (UN number, proper shipping name, class, packing group, quantity in liters), date of transport.
  2. Shipping Manifest or Delivery Receipt: For each compartment showing product type and quantity loaded.
  3. Truck Registration / OR/CR: Current registration documents.
  4. Insurance Certificate: Comprehensive insurance including third-party liability.
  5. Driver's License: Appropriate professional driver's license with HAZMAT endorsement (where required).
  6. Driver HAZMAT Training Certificate: Proof of current dangerous goods awareness training.

26.5 Emergency Response Information

The driver must carry, and be familiar with, the Emergency Response Guide for the products being carried. At minimum:

  • ERG Guide 128 (Flammable Liquids — Non-Polar / Water-Immiscible) for petroleum products.
  • Emergency actions: For any spill or leak: stop engine, eliminate ignition sources, evacuate area, call emergency services, and provide the product information (UN number) and truck details.
  • Spill containment: Use on-board spill kit (absorbent pads, booms, drain covers). Do not wash product into drains or stormwater systems.
  • First aid: For inhalation (move to fresh air), skin contact (remove clothing, wash with soap and water), eye contact (flush with water 15 minutes), ingestion (do NOT induce vomiting — call poison control).

26.6 Customer / Receiving Site Safety

  1. Follow all site-specific safety rules (posted at the entrance).
  2. Verify the product and quantity before unloading.
  3. Connect grounding cable to the designated grounding point before opening any hatch or valve.
  4. Use only approved hoses and fittings for the product being unloaded.
  5. Chock wheels before disconnecting any hoses.
  6. Stay with the vehicle during the entire unloading process — never leave a truck unattended while it is connected for loading or unloading.
  7. Report any product loss, overfill, or leak immediately to the site supervisor and your dispatcher.
