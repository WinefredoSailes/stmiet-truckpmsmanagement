Volume 3 — Preventive Maintenance

Chapter 6: PM Scheduling — Interval Types

6.1 Purpose of Preventive Maintenance

The Preventive Maintenance program ensures that every tanker truck in the fleet receives timely, scheduled service to prevent unexpected breakdowns, extend equipment life, maintain safety compliance, and comply with depot access requirements. A well-executed PM program reduces costly emergency repairs and keeps the fleet operational.

6.2 Interval Types

Each PM task in the system is assigned one of four interval types that determines when it becomes due:

TypeIconBasisExample
MILEAGE🛣️Odometer reading"Oil change every 5,000 km" — due when current_mileage ≥ last_mileage + 5,000
HOURS⏱️Engine hours meter"Belt inspection every 500 hrs" — due when current_hours ≥ last_hours + 500
CALENDAR📅Elapsed time in days"Tank hydrostatic test every 365 days" — due when today ≥ last_done + 365 days
VISUAL👁️Manual judgment"Pre-trip inspection" — always shows as "visual" status; no automatic calculation

6.3 Due Date Calculation

The system calculates PM status in real-time (no stored "due date" field). The formula for each interval type:

  • MILEAGE: current_mileage >= last_mileage_km + interval_value → DUE. A 10% threshold applies: if within 10% of interval, status shows as "Due" (yellow) instead of "Overdue."
  • HOURS: Same logic as mileage, using engine hours.
  • CALENDAR: today >= last_completed_at + timedelta(days=interval_value) → DUE. Same 10% grace threshold.
  • VISUAL: Always returns "visual" status — no auto-calculation.

6.4 Status Colors & Meanings

StatusColorMeaningAction Required
OKGreenWithin service intervalNone — monitor at next interval
DUEYellowWithin 10% of interval thresholdSchedule service within the week
OVERDUERedPast the interval thresholdService immediately — do not dispatch until resolved
VISUALBlueManual inspection requiredInspect before next dispatch
NO DATAGrayNever completed, no baseline setPerform initial service to establish baseline

6.5 How PM Schedules Update on Job Order Close

When a Job Order is closed, the system automatically:

  1. Records the truck's current mileage and engine hours at the time of close.
  2. Finds the relevant PM Schedule(s) that match the line items on the Job Order (matched by task template).
  3. Updates those PM Schedules:
    • last_completed_at = JO close timestamp
    • last_mileage_km = mileage at close
    • last_engine_hours = engine hours at close
    • status recalculated automatically
  4. Line items without a matching PM template do not update any schedule.
Important: PM schedules can also be updated manually. Click the pencil icon next to any PM schedule on the truck detail page to edit the last-completed values directly. This is useful when service was performed outside the Job Order system (e.g., third-party shop).

Chapter 8: Step-by-Step PM Procedures

This chapter contains the complete step-by-step procedures for all 23 PM categories. Each procedure follows a standardized format with safety precautions, required tools, detailed steps, acceptance criteria, and documentation requirements.

All procedures assume the following General Safety Rules apply at all times:

  • Wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): safety glasses, steel-toe boots, gloves, and high-visibility vest.
  • Ensure the truck is parked on level ground with the parking brake engaged.
  • Chock the wheels before working under or around the truck.
  • For tanker trucks: confirm the compartment is empty and gas-free before any hot work or entry.
  • Use lockout/tagout (LOTO) procedures when working on electrical, hydraulic, or pneumatic systems.
  • Do not work alone on hazardous tasks — always have a spotter or second mechanic present.

8.1 Engine Oil & Filter Change

Engine Oil and Filter Replacement

Purpose: Remove contaminated engine oil and replace with fresh oil of the correct grade. Replace oil filter to maintain proper lubrication and engine life.
Scope: All diesel-powered tanker trucks
Frequency: Every 5,000 km or 250 engine hours, whichever comes first
PPE Required: Safety glasses, nitrile or latex gloves, steel-toe boots, coveralls
Tools & Materials: Socket wrench set, oil filter wrench (strap-type or cap-type), drain pan (15L+ capacity), funnel, torque wrench, rags, oil absorbent pads, new oil filter, correct grade engine oil (as specified by manufacturer, typically 15W-40 or 20W-50 for diesel), replacement drain plug gasket
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Park truck on level ground, engage parking brake, chock wheels.
  2. Warm up engine for 2-3 minutes to thin oil for better drainage (oil should be warm, not hot).
  3. Turn off engine.
  4. Position drain pan under the oil drain plug.
  5. Using appropriate socket, loosen and remove drain plug.
  6. Allow oil to drain completely (5-10 minutes).
  7. Inspect drain plug gasket — replace if crushed or damaged.
  8. While oil drains, move drain pan under oil filter.
  9. Using oil filter wrench, loosen filter by turning counterclockwise.
  10. Oil will spill — position rags to catch drips.
  11. Remove filter completely.
  12. Wipe the filter mounting surface on the engine block with a clean rag.
  13. Apply a thin coat of fresh oil to the new filter's rubber gasket.
  14. Screw new filter onto mounting stud by hand until gasket contacts the mounting surface.
  15. Tighten an additional 3/4 turn by hand (do not overtighten).
  16. Reinstall drain plug with new gasket.
  17. Torque to manufacturer specification (typically 30-50 Nm for M14 plugs).
  18. Refill engine with fresh oil through fill port.
  19. Check capacity: typically 15-20 liters for medium-duty diesel trucks.
  20. Pour 80% of expected capacity, then check dipstick.
  21. Add remaining oil to reach FULL mark.
  22. Start engine and run for 30 seconds.
  23. Check for leaks around drain plug and filter.
  24. Stop engine.
  25. Wait 2 minutes for oil to settle.
  26. Recheck dipstick and top off if necessary.
  27. Record mileage, oil grade and quantity used, and date.
Acceptance Criteria: Oil level at FULL mark on dipstick after warm-up cycle. No leaks at drain plug or filter after 30-second run. Oil pressure gauge reads normal (typically 40-60 psi at idle when warm).
⚠ SAFETY: Hot oil can cause severe burns. Allow engine to cool slightly (2-3 minutes) before draining. Oil is an environmental hazard — collect all waste oil in approved containers for recycling. Never drain oil onto the ground or into drains.
Tip: Keep a log of oil consumption between changes. If the truck requires more than 1L top-up between changes, investigate for leaks or engine wear.
Documentation: Record in Job Order line item: mileage, oil grade/viscosity, quantity used, filter part number. Update PM schedule for Engine Oil & Filter.

8.2 Coolant System — Flush & Refill

Coolant System Flush and Refill

Purpose: Remove contaminated or degraded coolant, flush the cooling system, and refill with fresh coolant at the correct concentration to prevent overheating, cavitation, and freeze damage.
Scope: All liquid-cooled diesel truck engines
Frequency: Every 40,000 km or 2 years, whichever comes first (or per manufacturer specification)
PPE Required: Safety glasses, heat-resistant gloves, coveralls, face shield (when opening hot system)
Tools & Materials: Drain pan (20L+), radiator flush chemical (optional), distilled water, premixed coolant (typically 50/50 concentrate/distilled water), funnel, coolant hydrometer or refractometer, hose, rags, replacement radiator cap, replacement drain plug gasket if applicable
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Ensure engine is cold (coolant temperature below 40°C).
  2. Place drain pan under radiator drain plug (typically at bottom corner of radiator).
  3. Slowly open radiator cap to release any residual pressure.
  4. Remove drain plug and allow coolant to drain completely.
  5. Close drain plug.
  6. If using flush chemical: fill system with water + flush chemical per manufacturer instructions.
  7. Run engine with heater on HIGH for 10-15 minutes.
  8. Drain again.
  9. Refill with clean distilled water, run engine 5 minutes, drain again (repeat until draining water is clear).
  10. Close drain plug.
  11. If not using chemical flush: fill with distilled water, run engine 10 min, drain.
  12. Repeat until draining water is clear.
  13. Close drain plug.
  14. Inspect all coolant hoses for cracks, soft spots, or swelling.
  15. Replace if compromised.
  16. Inspect radiator cap gasket and pressure seal.
  17. Replace if worn.
  18. Mix coolant concentrate with distilled water to achieve 50/50 ratio (or as specified for your climate — 60/40 for extreme cold).
  19. Fill radiator through cap opening until full.
  20. Fill recovery tank to COLD FILL line.
  21. Install radiator cap tightly.
  22. Run engine with heater on HIGH until thermostat opens (upper radiator hose becomes hot).
  23. Check for leaks at hoses, water pump, drain plug, and radiator seams.
  24. Stop engine and allow to cool.
  25. Recheck coolant level and top off as needed.
Acceptance Criteria: Coolant free of contamination (no rust, oil, or debris). Coolant concentration 50% ±5% (tested with refractometer — reading 1.10-1.14 SG for ethylene glycol). No leaks at any connection. Radiator cap holds pressure (test with pressure tester if available). Recovery tank at correct level.
⚠ SAFETY: Coolant is toxic (ethylene glycol). Do not discharge into drains or onto ground. Collect all waste coolant in sealed containers for proper disposal. Never open a hot cooling system — severe scalding will occur. Wait until engine is cold (at least 1 hour after shutdown).
Tip: Use distilled water, not tap water. Tap water contains minerals that cause scale buildup and reduce cooling efficiency. For trucks with Cummins or Caterpillar engines, use SCA (Supplemental Coolant Additive) test strips to verify cavitation protection.
Documentation: Record mileage, coolant type/concentration, quantity used, date performed.

8.3 Radiator & Hose Inspection

Radiator and Coolant Hose Inspection

Purpose: Inspect radiator core, tanks, fins, and all coolant hoses for leaks, damage, blockages, or deterioration that could lead to overheating.
Scope: All trucks
Frequency: Every 10,000 km or 6 months
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves
Tools & Materials: Flashlight, pressure tester (cooling system), inspection mirror, rag, coolant (for top-off)
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. With engine cold, remove radiator cap and inspect cap gasket and seal.
  2. Visually inspect radiator core for bent or damaged fins.
  3. Use flashlight to check for debris blockages between fins (bugs, mud, leaves).
  4. Inspect radiator seams (where plastic tank meets aluminum core) for white or pink deposits indicating seepage.
  5. Check upper and lower radiator hoses for: cracks, soft spots (collapsible under squeeze), bulging, chafing on fan shroud, oil or fuel contamination (swollen hose).
  6. Check heater hoses (firewall connections) for same defects.
  7. Check all hose clamp connections.
  8. Confirm clamps are tight, not over-tightened (cutting into hose), and positioned past the hose barb.
  9. Inspect coolant recovery tank: check for cracks, verify hose connection to radiator overflow nipple is secure and clear.
  10. Pressure test: install pressure tester on radiator neck, pump to system pressure rating (typically 12-16 psi for trucks).
  11. Watch gauge for 2 minutes — pressure should hold steady.
  12. If pressure drops, locate leak by listening for hissing and inspecting all connections with a mirror.
  13. Relieve pressure and remove tester.
  14. Top off coolant if needed.
  15. Install radiator cap.
Acceptance Criteria: No leaks detected under pressure test (pressure holds for 2 minutes). Hoses firm but pliable — no cracks, soft spots, or swelling. Clamps secure and correctly positioned. Radiator fins clean and undamaged (at least 90% open).
⚠ SAFETY: Do not pressure test a hot cooling system. Ensure engine is cold.
Tip: Squeeze-test hoses: a good hose feels firm but springy. A hose that feels mushy or collapses easily is showing internal degradation and should be replaced preemptively.
Documentation: Record inspection findings, pressure test result (PSI held / duration), any hoses replaced.

8.4 Fan Belt & Tensioner Inspection

Fan Belt (Serpentine/V-Belt) and Tensioner Inspection

Purpose: Inspect condition and tension of all drive belts and the automatic tensioner to prevent belt failure, loss of cooling, alternator output, and power steering.
Scope: All trucks with belt-driven accessories
Frequency: Every 10,000 km or 6 months
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves (avoid loose clothing near moving parts)
Tools & Materials: Belt tension gauge (optional), 12mm/14mm socket, pry bar (for manual tensioners), flashlight
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Turn off engine and remove key.
  2. Visually inspect belt(s) along entire length: look for cracks on the ribbed surface, glazing (shiny, hardened surface), fraying on edges, missing chunks of rubber, separation between layers.
  3. Check belt routing against diagram on fan shroud or owner manual.
  4. Confirm belt is correctly seated in all pulley grooves.
  5. Check automatic tensioner: inspect for wobble or misalignment (indicates bearing wear).
  6. Check tensioner pulley for smooth rotation — spin by hand; should turn freely without grinding or wobble.
  7. Measure belt deflection: push down on belt at longest unsupported span.
  8. Acceptable deflection: 10-15mm (3/8 to 5/8 inch) for typical truck belts.
  9. For vehicles with automatic tensioner: verify indicator mark is within the acceptable range on the tensioner body.
  10. For manual tensioner systems: adjust as needed by loosening mounting bolt and pivoting alternator or accessory.
  11. Check accessory pulleys (alternator, water pump, AC compressor, power steering): spin each by hand.
  12. Feel for roughness, looseness, or wobble.
  13. Check pulley alignment: lay a straightedge across pulleys — all pulley grooves should align within 1mm.
Acceptance Criteria: Belt shows no cracks, glazing, fraying, or chunking. Tensioner indicator in acceptable range (or deflection within 10-15mm). All pulleys spin smoothly without wobble. Belt properly seated in all grooves.
⚠ SAFETY: Never inspect belts with engine running. Loose clothing, hair, or tools can be pulled into rotating components. Ensure engine is off and key is removed.
Tip: If one belt shows significant wear, replace all belts in the system simultaneously. A failing tensioner often damages the new belt prematurely.
Documentation: Record belt condition, tension measurement, any replacements (belt part numbers).

8.5 Brake Lining Inspection

Brake Lining (Brake Shoe Pad) Inspection

Purpose: Inspect brake lining thickness, condition, and contact pattern on all wheels. Determine if replacement is needed before lining material is critically low.
Scope: All trucks — inspect all wheels (typically 6-10 wheels per truck)
Frequency: Every 10,000 km or 3 months for severe service
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves, steel-toe boots, hearing protection (if using air tools)
Tools & Materials: Tire chocks, jack stands (minimum 6-ton capacity), wheel chocks, lug wrench, torque wrench, brake spoon or adjustment tool, ruler or brake lining thickness gauge, flashlight, penetrating oil, air gun (for dust removal — use with dust collection)
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Chock wheels on axles that will remain on ground.
  2. Loosen lug nuts slightly while truck is on ground.
  3. Jack up truck and place jack stands under frame rails at specified lifting points — never work under a truck supported only by a jack.
  4. Remove wheels and set aside.
  5. Remove brake drum inspection plug (rubber plug on backing plate) or remove drum entirely.
  6. For drum brakes: Use brake spoon to back off the adjuster (turn in direction of arrow or counterclockwise) to release brake shoes from drum.
  7. Pull drum straight off — may require penetrating oil on hub/drum mating surface if seized.
  8. Inspect drum inner surface (see Section 8.6 for drum inspection).
  9. Measure lining thickness at both ends of each shoe (leading and trailing).
  10. Compare thickest and thinnest points.
  11. Replace if thickness is ≤ 3mm (service limit) at any point.
  12. For disc brakes (if equipped): Remove caliper retaining pins or bolts.
  13. Swing caliper up or remove entirely (suspend with wire — do not let hang by hose).
  14. Remove brake pads from caliper.
  15. Measure pad friction material thickness — replace if ≤ 3mm.
  16. Check for uneven wear (tapered pads indicate caliper guide pin binding).
  17. Inspect rotor surface (see Section 8.6).
  18. Check brake hardware: return springs (drum), retaining clips, anti-rattle springs, caliper slide pins.
  19. Replace any corroded or weak hardware.
  20. Clean brake dust from backing plate and hub surface (use approved vacuum — avoid breathing brake dust).
  21. Reassemble: Install new or existing shoes/pads.
  22. For drum brakes: pre-adjust shoes before drum installation.
  23. For disc brakes: compress caliper piston using appropriate tool before reinstalling.
  24. Install wheel, tighten lug nuts in star pattern to manufacturer torque (typically 450-600 Nm for heavy trucks).
  25. Lower truck.
  26. Re-torque lug nuts after 50 km of driving.
Acceptance Criteria: Lining thickness ≥ 6mm minimum on friction material (replace at 3mm). Even contact pattern across entire lining surface (at least 80% contact). No oil or grease contamination on lining. All hardware present and functional.
⚠ SAFETY: Brake dust may contain asbestos. Never use compressed air to clean brake dust. Use HEPA vacuum or wet rag method. Always use jack stands rated for the vehicle weight. Never rely solely on a hydraulic jack.
Tip: Measure lining thickness at the thinnest point. If one wheel requires replacement, inspect both sides of that axle — uneven braking can cause dangerous pulling. Always replace brake shoes/pads in axle sets (both sides).
Documentation: Record lining thickness for each wheel position (LF, RF, LR, RR, etc.). Note any drums/rotors replaced. Record remaining thickness of replaced components.

8.6 Brake Drum / Disc Inspection

Brake Drum and Brake Disc (Rotor) Inspection

Purpose: Inspect braking surfaces for wear, scoring, cracks, heat damage, and out-of-round condition that affects braking performance.
Scope: All trucks — inspect each brake drum or disc during lining inspection
Frequency: Every brake service (lining replacement) or every 30,000 km
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves, steel-toe boots
Tools & Materials: Vernier caliper or drum micrometer, dial indicator with magnetic base (for runout), flashlight, 150-grit sandpaper (for minor surface rust)
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Drum Inspection: Clean drum interior surface with brake cleaner and rag.
  2. Visually inspect friction surface for severe scoring (grooves deeper than 1mm), heat checking (hairline cracks on surface), hard spots (discolored, shiny areas — "glazing"), and cracks extending to edge or bolt holes.
  3. Measure drum inside diameter at several points around circumference using drum micrometer or inside caliper.
  4. Compare to maximum allowable diameter (stamped on drum outer surface or manufacturer spec).
  5. If worn past maximum + 0.060 inch (1.5mm), replace drum.
  6. Check for out-of-round: measure diameter at multiple points around circumference; variation should be ≤ 0.005 inch (0.13mm).
  7. Disc (Rotor) Inspection: Clean rotor friction surface with brake cleaner.
  8. Visually inspect for scoring, cracks, heat discoloration (blue/purple indicates overheating), hard spots, and rust.
  9. Measure rotor thickness using micrometer at several points around circumference, about 10mm from outer edge.
  10. Minimum thickness is stamped on rotor edge — replace if at or below that value.
  11. Thickness variation between measurements should be ≤ 0.001 inch (0.025mm).
  12. Check rotor runout: mount dial indicator against rotor friction surface, rotate hub.
  13. Runout should be ≤ 0.004 inch (0.10mm).
  14. If excessive, check hub bearing play first (bearing wear can cause false runout).
Acceptance Criteria: Drum: inside diameter within manufacturer limit, no cracks, no severe scoring. Maximum out-of-round 0.005 inch (0.13mm). Disc: thickness above minimum, no cracks, no hard spots. Thickness variation ≤ 0.001 inch (0.025mm). Runout ≤ 0.004 inch (0.10mm).
⚠ SAFETY: Wear particulate respirator if cleaning with abrasives. Rotors can develop hard spots from overheating that cause braking vibration and reduced friction.
Tip: When replacing drums or rotors, always replace in axle pairs. New drums/rotors with old linings can cause uneven braking. Resurfacing is rarely cost-effective for heavy truck components — replacement is preferred.
Documentation: Record drum/rotor condition, measurements (diameter, thickness, runout), replacement decisions with part numbers.

8.7 Air Brake System Inspection

Air Brake System Inspection (DOT-compliant)

Purpose: Inspect the complete compressed air brake system including compressor, governor, air tanks, lines, chambers, slack adjusters, and brake valve to ensure safe stopping performance.
Scope: All trucks equipped with air brake systems
Frequency: Weekly or every 5,000 km
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves, hearing protection
Tools & Materials: Tire thumper or mallet, soapy water spray bottle, air pressure gauge, ruler, flashlight, crows foot wrench set (for slack adjuster), chocks
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Pre-test: Chock wheels.
  2. Ensure air system fully charged (governor cut-out at ~120-135 psi).
  3. Turn off engine.
  4. Leak Test: With engine off and full air pressure, note pressure reading.
  5. Observe pressure drop over 1 minute.
  6. With brakes released: maximum 2 psi drop per minute.
  7. With brakes applied (push pedal fully): maximum 3 psi drop per minute.
  8. If exceeds, locate leaks using soapy water at all fittings, chambers, and valve connections.
  9. Low Pressure Warning: With engine off, slowly drain air by depressing brake pedal repeatedly.
  10. Low pressure warning (buzzer + light) must activate before pressure drops below 60 psi (typically at 70-75 psi).
  11. Spring Brake Activation: Continue draining air.
  12. Spring brakes should mechanically apply (truck will not roll) when pressure drops to approximately 30-40 psi.
  13. Compressor & Governor: Start engine and let air pressure build.
  14. Governor cut-in should be approximately 100-110 psi.
  15. Governor cut-out at approximately 120-135 psi.
  16. Listen for abnormal compressor noise (knocking, continuous running).
  17. Air Tank Drain: Open each air tank drain valve (wet tank, primary, secondary) fully.
  18. Observe discharge for excessive moisture, oil contamination, or rust particles.
  19. Close valves once drained.
  20. Slack Adjuster Inspection: With brakes released, measure pushrod stroke at each brake chamber using ruler.
  21. Apply brakes fully (100 psi) and measure again.
  22. Stroke should be within limits: Type 20/24 chamber: 1.75 inch max.
  23. Type 30 chamber: 2.0 inch max.
  24. Adjust if stroke exceeds limit: hold adjusting nut (usually 9/16 or 5/8 inch), turn adjusting bolt counterclockwise until lining contacts drum, then back off 1/2 turn.
  25. Brake Chamber Inspection: Inspect chamber housing for cracks, corrosion holes, loose mounting bolts.
  26. Check pushrod for bent condition.
  27. Verify chamber type is same on both sides of axle.
  28. Hose & Tubing: Inspect all air hoses for chafing, cracks, bulges, or weather checking.
  29. Ensure hoses are properly supported and not contacting hot surfaces or moving parts.
  30. Check nylon tubing for embrittlement near connections.
Acceptance Criteria: Air pressure drop within limits (2 psi released, 3 psi applied). Low pressure warning activates above 60 psi. Governor cuts in at 100-110 psi, cuts out at 120-135 psi. No air leaks detected. Pushrod stroke within limits — automatic adjusters not over-extended. Air tanks drained — minimal moisture/oil present.
⚠ SAFETY: Never work on a air brake system without fully draining air pressure first. The energy stored in air tanks can cause sudden, violent movement of components. Chock wheels before any brake work. Do not attempt to disassemble spring brake chambers — they contain a powerful compressed spring that can cause fatal injuries. Spring brake disassembly requires a certified technician with specialized equipment.
Tip: If automatic slack adjusters consistently require manual adjustment, they may be at the end of their service life. Replace them rather than continuing to adjust. Check pushrod stroke on both sides of each axle — uneven stroke indicates a problem with the automatic adjuster on the longer-stroke side.
Documentation: Record: cut-in/cut-out pressures, pushrod stroke for each wheel position, any adjustments made, air dryer service interval status.

8.8 Tire Condition Inspection

Tire Condition Visual Inspection

Purpose: Inspect all tires for damage, irregular wear, proper inflation, and safety defects to prevent blowouts and ensure road safety.
Scope: All trucks — all tire positions including spare
Frequency: Daily (pre-trip) and every 5,000 km (detailed)
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves, high-visibility vest
Tools & Materials: Flashlight, tread depth gauge, tire pressure gauge (dual-foot type for steering and drive tires), hammer or tire thumper
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Visual Walk-Around: Walk completely around the truck inspecting each tire.
  2. Check for: under-inflation (bulging sidewalls, excessive sidewall flex), over-inflation (center tread wear, crowning).
  3. Use tire thumper to strike each tire — a properly inflated tire produces a solid, resonant sound; an under-inflated tire sounds dull.
  4. Sidewall Inspection: Inspect both sidewalls of each tire for: cuts, snags, bulges, blisters, exposed cords or belts, weather checking (fine cracks), abrasion damage.
  5. Tread Inspection: Look for: irregular wear patterns (cupping, feathering, shoulder wear, center wear), stones or debris embedded in tread (remove with scraper), foreign object penetration (nails, screws, metal), chunking or tearing of tread rubber.
  6. Valve & Cap: Check valve stem for cracks at the base.
  7. Confirm valve cap is present and tight (caps are secondary seals).
  8. Rim & Wheel Hardware: Inspect rim for cracks (especially around lug holes and weld joints).
  9. Check for bent or damaged rim flanges.
  10. Inspect all lug nuts — none should be loose, missing, or show signs of movement (rust ring around nut indicates loosening).
  11. Check for wheel cracks (especially on aluminum wheels around lug holes).
  12. Spare Tire: Verify spare tire is present, properly mounted, and inflated to recommended pressure.
Acceptance Criteria: No cuts, bulges, blisters, or exposed cords on any tire. Tread depth ≥ 2/32 inch (1.6mm) on all tires — 4/32 inch (3.2mm) minimum for steering axle tires in most jurisdictions. No irregular wear exceeding 2/32 inch (1.6mm) difference across the tread. Valve caps present on all tires. All lug nuts tight and properly torqued. No rim cracks or damage.
⚠ SAFETY: A tire blowout at highway speed can cause loss of vehicle control. Never attempt to repair a tire with sidewall damage, exposed cords, or bulges — replace immediately. Do not inflate a tire past the maximum pressure stamped on the sidewall.
Tip: Steering axle tires should always have the deepest tread and be the newest tires in the fleet. When replacing, always use matched tires on the same axle (same brand, model, tread depth within 2/32 inch). Never mix radial and bias-ply tires on the same axle.
Documentation: Record tire positions with significant wear or damage, tread depth measurements (steering and outer drive positions). Note any replacement tire serial numbers.

8.9 Tread Depth & Pressure Measurement

Tire Tread Depth and Pressure Measurement

Purpose: Quantitatively measure tread depth and inflation pressure on all tires to ensure compliance with legal minimums and optimal tire life.
Scope: All trucks — weekly for long-haul, daily for local operations
Frequency: Weekly or every 5,000 km
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves, high-visibility vest
Tools & Materials: Tread depth gauge (dial-type or digital), tire pressure gauge (calibrated), air compressor with tire chuck, valve core tool (for replacing cores)
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Pressure Check: Check tire pressures when tires are cold (driven less than 1 mile at moderate speed).
  2. Remove valve cap.
  3. Press gauge firmly onto valve stem and read pressure.
  4. Compare to the manufacturer's recommended pressure (stamped on sidewall or in vehicle placard).
  5. Steering tires: typically 100-120 psi.
  6. Drive tires: typically 90-110 psi.
  7. Add air if below recommended pressure.
  8. If a tire requires more than 10% of its pressure to fill, inspect for leaks (soapy water at valve core, bead area, and tread).
  9. Reinstall valve cap.
  10. Tread Depth Check: Insert tread depth gauge probe into the tread groove at a shallow area (usually near center or outer edge).
  11. Push gauge base flat against tread surface.
  12. Read depth in 32nds of an inch or millimeters.
  13. Measure at three positions across the tread (outer shoulder, center, inner shoulder) and at two points around the circumference (180 degrees apart).
  14. Record the lowest reading.
  15. Legal Minimums: Steer axle: 4/32 inch (3.2mm) minimum in most commercial regulations.
  16. Drive and trailer axles: 2/32 inch (1.6mm) minimum.
  17. Flag any tire below these limits for immediate replacement.
Acceptance Criteria: All tires within 5 psi of recommended cold pressure. Tread depth above legal minimums. No more than 2/32 inch (1.6mm) difference between tires on same axle. Spare tire at maximum recommended pressure.
⚠ SAFETY: Over-inflating a tire beyond its rated pressure can cause explosive failure. Use a tire cage when inflating a tire that has been run flat or shows sidewall damage. Split rim wheels require special handling — deflate completely before removing any hardware.
Tip: Tire pressure should be checked and logged consistently. A tire losing more than 2 psi per week has a slow leak that should be investigated. Under-inflation by just 20% reduces tire life by 25% and increases fuel consumption by 2-3%.
Documentation: Record pressures and tread depths for each tire position. Use consistent format: LF (left front), RF (right front), LDO (left drive outer), LDI (left drive inner), etc.

Overfill Prevention System (Vapor Recovery Compatible) Inspection

Purpose: Inspect and test the overfill prevention system to ensure it prevents tank compartment overfilling during loading, which could result in spillage, environmental damage, and safety hazards.
Scope: All petroleum tanker trucks with overfill prevention sensors/valves
Frequency: Monthly or per depot access requirements

Optical: indicator confirms detection. No physical damage, corrosion, or contamination on any sensor. Cable fully intact with good connector.
⚠ SAFETY: Petroleum products are flammable and toxic. Purge compartments before accessing internal sensors. No hot work within 10 meters of compartment openings. Use only non-sparking tools when working near tank openings.

8.11 Manlid & Gasket Inspection

Manlid Cover and Gasket Inspection

Purpose: Inspect each compartment's manlid cover, hinge, clamping mechanism, and gasket to ensure vapor-tight sealing and prevent product loss or contamination during transport.
Scope: All tanker trucks — inspect each compartment manlid individually
Frequency: Weekly or before every loading
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves, steel-toe boots
Tools & Materials: Flashlight, rag, gasket replacement kit (as needed), torque wrench
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Open each manlid fully and secure with safety prop (if equipped).
  2. Inspect gasket on lid underside: check for cuts, compression set (flattened permanently), brittleness, cracks, separation from lid channel.
  3. Inspect gasket channel for rust or debris buildup.
  4. Clean channel with rag if needed.
  5. Inspect lid sealing surface: must be clean, flat, free of dents or distortion.
  6. Check hinge pin for wear and lubrication.
  7. Verify hinge allows full opening (90+ degrees).
  8. Inspect clamping mechanism: check over-center latches or T-bolts for smooth operation.
  9. Check latch rollers for flat spots or wear.
  10. Check latch strike plates on tank body for deformation.
  11. Verify all clamps engage fully and hold lid firmly closed.
  12. Close manlid and engage clamping mechanism.
  13. Verify that the lid closes with moderate resistance (compressing the gasket).
  14. Check for gaps between lid and tank body — use feeler gauge if available.

  17. Pressurize tank vapor system to 1 psi using vapor recovery connection.
  18. Apply soapy water around each manlid perimeter.
  19. Look for bubbles indicating leaks.

8.14 Grounding & Static Discharge System Inspection

Grounding Cable and Static Discharge System Inspection

Purpose: Inspect the truck's grounding system to ensure a reliable electrical connection between the truck chassis and the depot grounding point, preventing static electricity buildup during loading operations.
Scope: All tanker trucks
Frequency: Weekly
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves
Tools & Materials: Multimeter (continuity function), flashlight, rag, file or emery cloth, dielectric grease
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Cable Inspection: Locate grounding cable(s) — typically a green/yellow striped cable with a clamp at one end, attached near the rear of the chassis or in a side cabinet.
  2. Inspect cable jacket for cuts, abrasion, corrosion.
  3. Check for broken strands at attachment points.
  4. Cable Route: Verify cable is routed without sharp bends, kinks, or contact with hot surfaces or moving components.
  5. Ensure cable length is sufficient to reach typical depot grounding points (minimum 3 meters).
  6. Grounding Clamp (Spring Clamp): Inspect clamp jaws for clean, bare metal contact surfaces.
  7. Remove any paint, rust, or contamination using file or emery cloth.
  8. Test spring tension — clamp should hold firmly on a 10mm steel test piece.
  9. Check clamp pivot for smooth operation.
  10. Inspect clamp-to-cable connection: verify it is mechanically secure and electrically continuous.
  11. Chassis Ground Connection: Trace cable to chassis attachment point.
  12. Verify bolt or stud is tight.
  13. Inspect connection for corrosion.
  14. Clean and apply dielectric grease if needed.
  15. Continuity Test: Set multimeter to continuity or resistance mode (200 ohms range).
  16. Place one probe on the end of the grounding clamp contact surface.
  17. Place the other probe on a clean, bare metal chassis point (scrape paint if needed).
  18. Resistance reading should be ≤ 5 ohms.
  19. If higher, check all connections and cable for damage.
  20. Stored Energy Test (optional but recommended): After truck has been driven, use a static discharge meter (if available) to measure static charge on the tank body before grounding.
  21. Reading should rapidly drop to near zero when clamp is attached.
Acceptance Criteria: Grounding cable: intact jacket, no broken strands, length sufficient. Clamp: clean bare-metal contact surface, strong spring tension. Chassis connection: tight, corrosion-free. Continuity: ≤ 5 ohms from clamp to chassis ground.
⚠ SAFETY: Static discharge is a leading cause of tanker truck fires during loading. Never begin loading without the grounding clamp securely attached to a verified grounding point. If the grounding system fails continuity test, tag the truck out of service immediately for repair.
Tip: The depot grounding system also requires inspection — but that is the depot's responsibility. Your job is to ensure the truck-side system is 100% reliable. Apply a thin coat of dielectric grease to the clamp contact surface after cleaning to prevent rapid re-corrosion.
Documentation: Record continuity reading (ohms), clamp condition, cable inspection result, any cleaning or repairs performed.

8.15 Product Piping & Valves Inspection

Product Piping, Internal Valves, and Discharge System Inspection

Purpose: Inspect all product-carrying piping, internal safety valves, discharge valves, and associated fittings for leaks, corrosion, and mechanical integrity.
Scope: All tanker trucks — inspect per compartment and common discharge line
Frequency: Monthly
PPE Required: Safety glasses, chemical-resistant gloves, steel-toe boots
Tools & Materials: Flashlight, small mirror, rag, leak detection spray (for non-product sections), torque wrench
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Internal Safety Valve (Foot Valve): Located at bottom of each compartment.
  2. Verify valve operator linkage (cable or rod) is intact and properly adjusted.
  3. Operate the emergency/pneumatic release handle at the rear of the truck — confirm each compartment's internal valve opens and closes correspondingly.
  4. Check for leaks at valve body (product seepage past valve seat).
  5. Product Piping (Bottom-Loading / Discharge): Visually inspect all product piping from each compartment's internal valve to the discharge outlet.
  6. Check for corrosion, dents, rubbing marks, weld cracks, and joint leaks.
  7. Pay special attention to pipe supports and areas where pipes pass through frame members.
  8. Check threaded connections at flanges and unions for seepage.
  9. Discharge Valve (Butterfly / Ball Valve): Operate each discharge valve fully open and closed.
  10. Valve should turn smoothly with moderate resistance (not too tight, not sloppy).
  11. Inspect valve body for cracks or corrosion.
  12. Verify valve position indicator is accurate (open/closed markings).
  13. Emergency Shutoff System: Inspect pneumatic or mechanical linkage from the emergency release at the rear of the truck to each internal valve.
  14. Confirm all connecting rods, cables, and levers are secure and properly adjusted.
  15. Test emergency shutoff by pulling the handle — all internal valves should close.
  16. Piping Supports: Verify all pipe clamps and brackets are present and tight.
  17. Look for rubber or plastic chafing strips where pipes contact frame or other components.
  18. Replace missing chafing protection.
Acceptance Criteria: Internal valves: open and close fully, no leakage past seat. Piping: no corrosion deeper than 10% of wall thickness, no dents restricting flow, no leaks at any joint. Discharge valves: smooth operation through full travel. Emergency shutoff: all internal valves close when handle is pulled. Piping properly supported with chafing protection intact.
⚠ SAFETY: Product piping contains flammable liquids. Any leak is a fire hazard and environmental violation. If you detect any leak, tag the compartment out of service immediately. Conduct repair only after compartment is purged and gas-free certified.
Tip: Corrosion often starts at the bottom of pipes where water accumulates. Pay extra attention to pipe low points and drain plugs. If a pipe shows significant corrosion, use an ultrasonic thickness gauge (if available) to measure remaining wall thickness.
Documentation: Record per-compartment inspection results (pass/fail), any leaks found and locations, valve operation condition, emergency shutoff test result.

8.16 Transfer Hoses & Couplings Inspection

Product Transfer Hoses and End Couplings Inspection

Purpose: Inspect all flexible product transfer hoses (loading/unloading hoses) and their end couplings for signs of wear, damage, deterioration, and leakage to ensure safe product transfer.
Scope: All tanker trucks with on-board hose racks
Frequency: Weekly visually; monthly detailed inspection
PPE Required: Safety glasses, chemical-resistant gloves
Tools & Materials: Flashlight, mirror, rag, hose gauge or caliper (for measuring cover thickness)
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Hose Cover Inspection: Uncoil or lay out hose full length on clean surface.
  2. Visually inspect the full circumference of the hose.
  3. Look for: cuts or gouges penetrating through the cover to reinforcement layers, abrasion wear (flat spots), blisters or bubbles (cover separation from reinforcement), chemical attack (soft or sticky spots, swelling), heat damage (hardened, cracked areas), weather checking (fine surface cracks), kinking or crushing damage.
  4. Hose Reinforcement: If cover damage is found, inspect the underlying reinforcement.
  5. Exposed fabric or wire braid requires immediate hose replacement.
  6. End Couplings: Inspect each coupling (male and female) for corrosion, cracks, or distortion.
  7. Check coupling retention — the coupling must be securely crimped or swaged to the hose.
  8. Look for evidence of coupling movement (gap between coupling ferrule and hose cover).
  9. Inspect coupling threads for damage (cross-threading, galling).
  10. Clean threads with wire brush if needed.
  11. Hose Markings: Verify hose is marked with: manufacturer name, date of manufacture, rated working pressure, and any applicable standards (API, EN, etc.).
  12. Confirm hose has not exceeded its recommended service life (typically 5-7 years from manufacture date, regardless of condition).
  13. Hose Storage: Inspect hose rack or storage area.
  14. Verify hose is stored on a clean surface or rack — never on bare ground.
  15. Check that hose is not draped over sharp edges or in contact with chemicals, oil, or heat sources.
  16. Pressure Test (annual): Hydrostatically test each hose at 1.5x rated working pressure.
  17. Hold pressure for 5 minutes.
  18. No leakage or hose movement at fitting.
Acceptance Criteria: No cuts exposing reinforcement. No blisters, soft spots, or swelling. Couplings: securely attached, clean threads, no cracks. Working pressure rating meets or exceeds system pressure (typically 150-300 psi for gravity/unloading hoses). Hose not past service life. Annual pressure test passed.
⚠ SAFETY: A hose failure during loading under pressure can cause a massive product spill with fire and environmental consequences. A hose failure while unloading at a customer site can cause serious injury. Never use a damaged hose — replace immediately regardless of cost.
Tip: Rotate hoses periodically (every 6 months) to distribute wear. Label each hose with an ID number and installation date. The leading cause of hose failure is abrasion from dragging on concrete. Use hose ramps or always lift rather than drag.
Documentation: Record hose ID, condition rating, manufacture date, test pressure, test result, date of next test due.

8.18 Battery & Alternator Inspection

Battery System and Alternator Charging Test

Purpose: Inspect batteries, cables, terminals, and alternator output to ensure reliable engine starting and electrical system operation.
Scope: All trucks
Frequency: Monthly or every 10,000 km
PPE Required: Safety glasses, acid-resistant gloves (for wet-cell batteries)
Tools & Materials: Digital multimeter, battery load tester (optional), terminal brush, baking soda solution (for cleaning), dielectric grease, flashlight, wrench set
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Battery Visual Inspection: Inspect battery case for cracks, bulging, or leaks.
  2. Check battery hold-down bracket — must be tight to prevent vibration damage.
  3. Check battery terminal posts for corrosion (white/green/blue powder) — clean if present using terminal brush and baking soda solution.
  4. Cable Inspection: Inspect both positive and negative battery cables from terminal to chassis/ground and to starter.
  5. Check for: frayed or broken strands at terminals, cracked insulation, corrosion under the insulation (green substance), loose terminal clamps.
  6. Battery Voltage Test (Resting): Engine off, all loads off.
  7. Set multimeter to DC volts (20V range).
  8. Measure voltage across battery terminals.
  9. 12.6V+ = fully charged.
  10. 12.4V = 75% charged.
  11. 12.2V = 50% charged.
  12. Below 12.0V = discharged — recharge and investigate cause.
  13. Battery Load Test: If battery voltage is low, perform load test.
  14. Apply load tester per manufacturer instructions (typically half the CCA rating for 15 seconds).
  15. Minimum voltage during load: 9.6V at room temperature (higher in cold).
  16. Alternator Output Test: Start engine.
  17. Measure voltage across battery terminals at idle: should read 13.8-14.5V (12V system).
  18. Rev engine to 1500 RPM — voltage should rise to 14.0-14.7V (regulation point).
  19. Turn on all electrical loads (headlights, AC, hazards) — voltage should remain above 13.0V.
  20. If voltage stays below 13.0V at idle with loads, alternator is undercharging.
  21. If voltage exceeds 15.0V, voltage regulator is failing (overcharging).
  22. Belt Check: Inspect alternator drive belt condition and tension.
  23. See Section 8.4 for belt procedure.
  24. Battery Water Level (Serviceable Batteries): If batteries have vent caps, remove caps and check electrolyte level in each cell.
  25. Level should cover the plates (typically 10-15mm above plates).
  26. Top off with distilled water only — never add acid.
  27. Parasitic Draw Test (if battery drains): With engine off, set multimeter to amps (10A range).
  28. Disconnect negative battery terminal and connect meter in series (meter between terminal and cable).
  29. Reading should be below 50mA.
  30. Higher indicates parasitic draw that needs diagnosis.
Acceptance Criteria: Resting voltage ≥ 12.4V (75%+ charge). Load test: maintains ≥9.6V under load. Alternator: 13.8-14.5V at idle, 14.0-14.7V at 1500 RPM. No corrosion on terminals. Cables intact, no fraying. Battery water covers plates (if serviceable). Parasitic draw ≤ 50mA.
⚠ SAFETY: Batteries contain explosive hydrogen gas. No smoking, sparks, or flames near batteries. Disconnect negative terminal first, connect positive first. When jump-starting, connect dead positive first, then good positive, then good negative (to chassis ground, not dead battery), then dead negative last. Reverse order for removal. Acid can cause severe burns — flush immediately with water if contact occurs.
Tip: For trucks with dual batteries (common for diesel trucks), test each battery individually. Replace batteries in pairs — mixing old and new batteries reduces the life of both. Log the installation date on each battery to track service life (typically 3-5 years).
Documentation: Record battery resting voltage, alternator output voltage at idle and 1500 RPM, any cleaning or topping off performed, battery replacement dates and CCA rating.

8.19 Starter & Starting Circuit Inspection

Starter Motor and Starting Circuit Wiring Inspection

Purpose: Inspect the starter motor, solenoid, and all associated wiring for conditions that could cause starting failure, high current draw, or electrical fire.
Scope: All trucks
Frequency: Every 20,000 km or when starting issues are observed
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves, insulated tools (when working near battery)
Tools & Materials: Digital multimeter, wrench set, flashlight, contact cleaner
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. Visual Inspection: Locate starter motor on engine bell housing.
  2. Inspect starter body for cracks, oil leaks, or impact damage.
  3. Check mounting bolts — must be tight.
  4. Inspect starter solenoid: check for loose or corroded small-wire connections at the "S" (start signal) and "I" (ignition bypass) terminals.
  5. Main Power Cable: Trace battery positive cable to starter solenoid large terminal.
  6. Check for: corrosion, loose nut, frayed cable at terminal, melted insulation (indicates high resistance connection causing heat), chafing along cable route.
  7. Ground Circuit: Check battery negative cable at engine block or chassis ground.
  8. Must be tight and corrosion-free.
  9. Also check secondary ground strap between engine and chassis (engine-to-body ground) — this is critical for starter current return path.
  10. Voltage Drop Test (Cranking Circuit): Disable ignition or fuel system to prevent start (for safety and isolation).
  11. Set multimeter to DC volts.
  12. Positive side drop: Place positive probe on battery positive post, negative probe on starter solenoid terminal.
  13. Crank engine and read voltage drop: should be ≤ 0.5V.
  14. Negative side drop: Place positive probe on starter housing (clean metal surface), negative probe on battery negative post.
  15. Crank engine: drop should be ≤ 0.2V.
  16. Higher drops indicate high resistance connections that need cleaning or cable replacement.
  17. Starter Current Draw Test (if available): Use inductive ammeter clamp.
  18. Crank engine and read current.
  19. Typical diesel starter: 200-400 amps initial, 100-200 amps cranking.
  20. Excessively high current may indicate bad starter, seized engine, or failing battery.
  21. Pinion Gear Check: If starter is removed for service, inspect the Bendix drive gear teeth for chipping or wear.
  22. Inspect ring gear teeth (access through starter opening) for damage.
  23. Lightly lubricate Bendix drive mechanism with high-temp grease (do not over-lubricate).
Acceptance Criteria: Starter: securely mounted, no physical damage. Cables: no corrosion, fraying, or melted insulation. Voltage drop: positive ≤ 0.5V, negative ≤ 0.2V. Engine cranks briskly. Current draw within normal range.
⚠ SAFETY: Disable the ignition system or fuel shutoff before performing cranking tests to prevent accidental engine start. The starter circuit carries several hundred amps — short circuits can cause arc flashes, burns, and fire. Use insulated tools. Never bypass the neutral safety switch on automatic transmissions.
Tip: A slow crank is most often caused by a bad connection (corrosion or loose terminal), not a bad starter. Always check voltage drops before replacing the starter. If the starter solenoid clicks but the engine does not crank, suspect the battery or main cable connection first.
Documentation: Record: visual condition, voltage drop measurements (V+ and V-), current draw reading (if measured).

8.22 Drivetrain — Differential, U-Joints, Wheel Bearings

Drivetrain System Inspection (Differential, U-Joints, Wheel Bearings)

Purpose: Inspect the complete drivetrain from transmission output to wheel hubs, checking for wear, lubrication condition, and mechanical integrity that could lead to catastrophic failure.
Scope: All trucks
Frequency: Every 20,000 km or 6 months
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves, hearing protection (road test)
Tools & Materials: Flashlight, pry bar, grease gun (with lithium complex or moly grease), torque wrench, 3/8-inch drive ratchet with extensions
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. U-Joint Inspection: With truck on level ground, transmission in neutral, parking brake OFF, chock all wheels.
  2. Crawl under truck.
  3. Inspect each U-joint (typically 2-3 on driveline: transmission output, carrier bearing, differential input).
  4. Grasp driveline near each U-joint and attempt to twist — there should be ZERO rotational play.
  5. Attempt to move driveline up and down sideways near U-joint — maximum acceptable play: 0.010 inch (0.25mm).
  6. Check U-joint retaining clips or strap bolts — must be tight.
  7. Look for rust-colored dust around U-joint cross (indicates wear).
  8. Grease each U-joint (if equipped with grease fitting) until fresh grease purges from all four seals.
  9. Center / Carrier Bearing: Inspect rubber cushion for cracking or deterioration.
  10. Check bearing for smooth rotation — spin by hand; should feel smooth with no roughness.
  11. Inspect mounting bracket for cracks.
  12. Differential Inspection: Check differential oil level: remove fill plug (top of differential housing).
  13. Oil should be level with the bottom of the fill hole.
  14. If low, top off with correct grade gear oil (typically 80W-90 or 85W-140 for severe service).
  15. Check oil condition: dip a clean finger into fill hole — oil should be clear to amber-brown.
  16. Milky, white, or grey oil indicates water contamination.
  17. Black, burnt-smelling oil indicates overheating.
  18. Inspect differential housing for leaks at: pinion seal (front, where driveline enters), axle seals (inner wheel ends), cover gasket, vent tube (must be clear and routed upward).
  19. Axle U-joints: If equipped with independent suspension, inspect half-shaft U-joints or CV joints.
  20. Check boots for tears and grease leakage.
  21. Road Test: Drive truck in a straight line on a smooth road at 30-40 km/h.
  22. Listen for: whining or howling from differential (bearing or gear wear), clicking from U-joints (worn cross bearings), clunking on acceleration/deceleration (excessive driveline backlash), vibration at specific speeds (driveline balance or worn carrier bearing).
  23. While driving, gently turn left and right — listen for clicking from front CV joints (if equipped).
Acceptance Criteria: U-joints: zero rotational play, minimal lateral play (<0.010 inch). Carrier bearing: smooth rotation, no cracked rubber cushion. Differential: oil level correct, oil clean, no leaks. Road test: no unusual noises or vibrations.
⚠ SAFETY: A driveline failure at highway speed can separate from the vehicle, causing loss of control and potential injury. If any U-joint shows significant play or roughness, replace immediately. A driveline vibration that suddenly worsens may indicate imminent bearing failure.
Tip: Most driveline vibration issues are caused by worn U-joints rather than out-of-balance shafts. Check U-joints first. When replacing U-joints, replace all on the same driveline (transmission output and both differential ends). Use only grease-able U-joints with a proper grease schedule.
Documentation: Record: U-joint condition, differential oil level and condition, any leakage found, grease points serviced, road test results.

8.23 Water Brake System Inspection

Water Brake (Retarder) System Inspection

Purpose: Inspect the water brake hydraulic retarder system, including pump, valve, lines, and cooler, to ensure it provides supplemental braking power for heavy loads on grades.
Scope: Trucks equipped with water brake / hydraulic retarder systems
Frequency: Monthly or every 10,000 km
PPE Required: Safety glasses, gloves, hearing protection
Tools & Materials: Flashlight, wrenches, rag, coolant refractometer (if water brake shares engine coolant)
Step-by-Step Procedure:
  1. System Overview: Identify the water brake components: hydraulic pump (usually on front of engine or PTO-driven), control valve (cab-mounted or chassis-mounted), lines/hoses, heat exchanger (radiator or shell-and-tube type), and actuation cylinder (driveline or transmission-mounted).
  2. Fluid Level Check: Locate the hydraulic fluid reservoir.
  3. Check fluid level — should be between MIN and MAX marks.
  4. Fluid should be clear amber (hydraulic oil) or green (approved coolant, depending on system type).
  5. If fluid is milky, dark, or smells burnt, a complete fluid change is needed.
  6. Note the fluid type required (typically AW-32 or AW-46 hydraulic oil, or proprietary fluid).
  7. Pump Inspection: Inspect pump mounting bolts — must be tight.
  8. Check pump drive coupling for wear (if accessible).
  9. Inspect pump shaft seal for leaks.
  10. Listen for pump operation with engine at idle — should produce a steady, smooth sound without cavitation (rattling/gravelly sound indicates low fluid or air in system).
  11. Hose and Line Inspection: Trace all hydraulic lines from pump to control valve to heat exchanger to actuation cylinder.
  12. Inspect for: chafing, cracks, bulges, leaks at fittings, collapsed sections (restricted flow).
  13. Pay special attention to areas near exhaust or other heat sources.
  14. Heat Exchanger / Cooler: Inspect for debris blockage on fins (if air-cooled).
  15. Check for coolant leaks (if water-cooled via engine system).
  16. Verify cooler mounting brackets are intact.
  17. Control Valve: Test control lever or switch in cab — must move through full range smoothly.
  18. Verify valve linkage is properly adjusted (cable or rod).
  19. Inspect valve body for external leaks.
  20. Actuator / Caliper: Inspect actuation cylinder — check for leaks at rod seal, check for smooth rod movement.
  21. If water brake acts on a driveline disc, inspect the disc surface for scoring, heat checking, or warpage.
  22. Check disc thickness and compare to minimum specification.
  23. Test Operation: In a safe area with adequate stopping distance, drive truck to 30 km/h and apply water brake (50% engagement).
  24. Truck should decelerate smoothly without pulling or vibration.
  25. Overheating after 1 minute of continuous use at 50% engagement is normal — full engagement should not be held for more than 30 seconds.
  26. Verify that the system disengages fully when control is released.
Acceptance Criteria: Fluid level correct and fluid condition good. No leaks at any connection. Pump operates smoothly without unusual noise. Hoses intact with no chafing or bulges. Control linkage operates through full range smoothly. Braking: smooth deceleration without pulling or vibration. System disengages fully on release.
⚠ SAFETY: Water brake systems can generate extreme heat during sustained use. If the red warning light or temperature gauge indicates overheating, release the brake and allow system to cool. Do not touch hydraulic components immediately after heavy use — they can be hot enough to cause burns.
Tip: The water brake is a supplemental braking system only. It is not a parking brake. Always use the spring parking brake for parking. For trucks hauling heavy loads in mountainous terrain, the water brake should be applied continuously to maintain speed on downgrades, preserving the service brakes for emergency stops.
Documentation: Record fluid level, condition, any leaks repaired, heat exchanger condition, road test result.

Chapter 10: Initial Assessment & Triage

10.1 Receiving a Truck for Repair

When a truck arrives at the workshop with a reported problem, follow this standard intake procedure:

  1. Driver Report: Interview the driver or the person reporting the issue. Ask the five key questions:
    • What is the problem? (be specific — what does it do, or not do?)
    • When did it start? (sudden or gradual onset?)
    • Under what conditions does it happen? (speed, load, temperature, gear, turning?)
    • Are there any warning lights? (which ones, when did they come on?)
    • Any recent repairs or changes? (what was touched last?)
  2. Visual Walk-Around: Walk around the truck and look for obvious signs: fluid leaks (color, location, amount), damaged components, loose or hanging parts, abnormal tire wear, broken lights, damaged hoses.
  3. Fluid Check: Check all fluid levels and conditions — engine oil, coolant, transmission oil, power steering, brake fluid (if applicable), windshield washer. Note any fluid that is low, contaminated, or has an unusual odor.
  4. Functional Check: With the engine running (if safe), verify: warning lights on instrument panel, unusual noises (knocking, squealing, hissing, grinding), unusual smells (burning oil, fuel, rubber, electrical), brake function (pedal feel, air pressure buildup and leak-down rate), steering response (free play, pulling).
  5. Diagnostic Scan: Connect a diagnostic scan tool (if available) to check for stored fault codes. Record all active and historic codes.
  6. Triage Decision: Based on the initial assessment, classify the repair:
    • Emergency / Safety-Critical: Truck cannot be operated safely. Must be repaired before any movement. Examples: brake failure, steering failure, significant fuel/oil leak, tire blowout, no lights at night.
    • Routine: Truck can operate but should be scheduled for repair within the week. Examples: minor oil leak, worn belt, one burned-out marker light, small air leak.
    • Deferred: Issue does not affect safety or operation but should be monitored and addressed at the next scheduled PM. Examples: minor cosmetic damage, slight oil seepage from gasket, worn but not critical tire.

10.2 Repair vs. Replace Decision

For any failed or worn component, evaluate the following factors before deciding whether to repair or replace:

FactorFavor RepairFavor Replace

Rule of thumb: When in doubt on safety-critical components (brakes, steering, suspension, tires, wheel bearings), replace rather than repair. The cost difference is small compared to the consequences of a field failure.

Chapter 13: Quality Control & Pre-Return Inspection

13.1 Purpose of Quality Control

Before any truck is returned to service after a repair or PM, a quality control inspection must be performed. This ensures:

  • All work was completed correctly and safely.
  • No tools, parts, or materials were left in or on the truck.
  • All fasteners are properly tightened.
  • All fluids are at correct levels.
  • No new issues were introduced during the repair.
  • The truck is safe to operate on public roads.

13.2 QC Inspection Checklist

Responsible Role: Lead Mechanic or Supervisor (not the same person who performed the work)

  1. Walk-around inspection: Walk completely around the truck. Look for: tools or rags left on fenders, steps, or cab roof, loose parts or fasteners, fluid leaks (any new drips from areas that were worked on), component alignment (hoses, wiring, brackets not rubbing or contacting), all access panels and covers reinstalled and secured.
  2. Under-hood / Under-truck check: Verify all connections are tight (hose clamps, electrical connectors, bolts). Check that all fluid caps/dipsticks are installed and secure. Remove any rags, gloves, or trash left in the engine compartment or cab. Confirm that all removed guards and shields are reinstalled.
  3. Functional test: Start engine and let idle for 2-3 minutes. Check for: unusual noises from worked-on areas, warning lights (all should be off after initial bulb check), abnormal smoke from exhaust, fluid leaks at connections that were loosened. Test the systems that were repaired: brake application and release (if brakes were touched), steering lock-to-lock (if steering components were touched), light operation (if electrical was touched), horn, windshield wipers.
  4. Road test (if applicable): Drive the truck in a safe area. Check for: proper operation of the repaired system (e.g., brakes stop straight, transmission shifts smoothly, engine runs without missing), abnormal noises, vibrations, or smells, gauges reading normally (temperature, oil pressure, air pressure).
  5. Documentation check: Verify all line items are marked Done (or Not Applicable). Verify all parts used have been entered, with correct quantities and costs. Verify the actual hours entered are reasonable and match the work performed. Ensure the Job Order notes field contains any important observations or follow-up recommendations.
⚠ Critical: If the QC inspection reveals any deficiency, the truck must NOT be returned to service until the issue is corrected. The mechanic who performed the work must re-address the deficiency and the QC must be repeated. No exceptions — even for "minor" issues.

13.3 Common QC Failures & How to Prevent Them

Chapter 14: Job Order Close-out & Service Logging

14.1 When to Close a Job Order

A Job Order should be closed only when all of the following are true:

  1. All line items are marked Done or Not Applicable.
  2. All parts used have been entered into the system.
  3. All actual hours have been recorded for each line item.
  4. QC inspection has been passed.
  5. Road test (if needed) has been completed successfully.
  6. The truck is ready to return to service.

14.2 Closing a Job Order — Step by Step

Responsible Role: Admin, Staff, or Super Admin only (not the mechanic performing the work)

  1. Open the Job Order detail page.
  2. Review all line items and parts entries for completeness and accuracy.
  3. Click the "Close Job Order" button.
  4. A confirmation dialog appears explaining that closing is irreversible — read and confirm.
  5. The close form opens showing:
    • Current Mileage: Pre-filled from the truck's current odometer reading. Verify and adjust if needed (the truck may have been driven since the reading was last updated).
    • Current Engine Hours: Pre-filled from the truck's current hour meter. Verify.
    • Notes: Optional closing notes — summarize the work performed, any follow-up recommendations, or parts that need to be ordered for the next service.
  6. Click "Close Job Order" to finalize.

14.3 What Happens Automatically on Close

When a Job Order is closed, the system performs the following actions automatically:

  1. Status changes to CLOSED. The JO becomes read-only.
  2. Truck's mileage and engine hours are updated from the values entered on the close form.
  3. Any open line items are automatically marked as Done (with a note "Auto-completed on JO close").
  4. Service Log Entries are created in the immutable service ledger for:
    • Each line item that was completed.
    • The JO creation and close events.
  5. PM Schedule update: Any line item that was linked to a PM template will update the corresponding PM schedule with:
    • last_completed_at = close timestamp.
    • last_mileage_km = mileage at close.
    • last_engine_hours = hours at close.
    • The next due date is recalculated automatically.

14.4 Service Ledger — Immutable Audit Trail

The Service Ledger is a permanent, unchangeable record of every action performed. It serves as:

  • Legal record of maintenance performed on each truck.
  • Audit trail for compliance with depot access requirements.
  • Historical reference for recurring issues on a specific truck.
  • Mechanic accountability — each entry is tied to the person who performed the work.

Accessing the Service Ledger:

  • Full Ledger: Sidebar → Service Ledger (Admin+). Shows all entries, filterable by truck and action type.
  • Per-Truck Ledger: Open any truck detail page → Click "Full Ledger" button in the Service History section.
Important: The Service Ledger cannot be edited or deleted — not even by the Super Admin through the Django Admin interface. This immutability is a deliberate design feature to ensure the integrity of the maintenance record. If an error is discovered in a closed JO, a new JO must be created to correct it, and the original record remains as-is.

14.5 Reopening a Closed Job Order (Not Possible)

Once a Job Order is closed, it cannot be reopened. If additional work is needed on the same truck:

  1. Create a new Job Order for the new work.
  2. In the description, reference the original JO number: "Continuation of work from JO-2026-0042 — unresolved brake pull issue."
  3. This maintains a clean audit trail and prevents data integrity issues.

14.6 Archived / Historical Job Orders

Closed Job Orders remain visible in the Job Orders list with a "Closed" status. Use the status filter to view only closed JOs when researching history. The system does not automatically archive or delete old JOs — data is retained indefinitely for historical reporting.

Chapter 24: General Workshop Safety & PPE

24.1 The Safety Culture Principle

Safety is not a set of rules — it is a mindset. Every mechanic, driver, and supervisor in this organization is expected to:

  • Stop any task that feels unsafe, without fear of reprisal.
  • Speak up when they see an unsafe act or condition involving anyone.
  • Lead by example — wear proper PPE even for "quick" tasks.
  • Report every near-miss — a near-miss today prevents an accident tomorrow.
⚠ Golden Rule: If you cannot do a job safely, do not do it at all. No job is so urgent that it cannot be done safely.

24.2 General Workshop Safety Rules

The following rules apply at all times within the workshop, depot, or any location where truck maintenance is performed:

  1. Housekeeping: Keep all work areas clean and organized. Clean up spills immediately. Return tools to their designated storage after use. Do not leave tools, parts, or debris on the floor — they are tripping hazards. Dispose of rags contaminated with oil, fuel, or solvent in approved fire-safe containers.
  2. Spill Containment: Any spill of oil, fuel, coolant, or other fluids must be contained immediately using absorbent pads or granular absorbent. Report spills over 5 liters to the supervisor. Do not wash spills down drains — use proper disposal procedures.
  3. Ventilation: Ensure adequate ventilation when working with solvents, paints, welding equipment, or running engines indoors. Carbon monoxide from running engines is lethal in enclosed spaces. Use exhaust extraction hoses for all running engines.
  4. Fire Prevention: No smoking in the workshop. Store flammable liquids (fuel, solvents, paints) in approved fire-rated cabinets. Keep ignition sources away from battery charging areas (hydrogen gas). Know the location of all fire extinguishers and emergency exits.
  5. Compressed Air Safety: Never use compressed air to clean clothing, skin, or hair — air can enter the bloodstream through small cuts and cause fatal embolisms. Never point an air nozzle at another person. Use pressure regulators — do not exceed rated tool pressure.
  6. Confined Space: Tank compartments, pits, and storage tanks are confined spaces. NEVER enter a confined space without: a confined space permit, atmospheric testing (oxygen, LEL, toxic gas), continuous ventilation, a safety harness and retrieval line, and a trained attendant outside the space.
  7. Hot Work: Welding, grinding, cutting, or any activity that produces sparks requires a hot work permit. Clear the area of flammable materials within 10 meters. Have a fire watcher with an extinguisher present. Monitor for 30 minutes after work is completed.
  8. Drugs and Alcohol: Working under the influence of drugs or alcohol is strictly prohibited. Prescription medications that cause drowsiness must be reported to the supervisor before starting work.
  9. Personal Conduct: Horseplay, running, and practical jokes in the workshop are prohibited. Use designated walkways. Do not jump off loading docks or truck beds.
  10. Reporting: All injuries, no matter how minor, must be reported to the supervisor and recorded in the incident log. All near-misses must be reported and investigated.

24.5 Proper Tool Handling & Safety

24.5.1 Hand Tools — General Rules

  1. Use the right tool for the job. Never use a wrench as a hammer, a screwdriver as a pry bar, or pliers as a wrench. Using the wrong tool damages both the tool and the fastener, and increases injury risk.
  2. Inspect before use. Check wrenches for cracked jaws, sockets for worn drive ends, screwdrivers for mushroomed tips, hammers for loose heads. Any damaged tool must be removed from service and tagged for repair or disposal.
  3. Pull — do not push. Whenever possible, pull a wrench toward your body rather than pushing away. If you must push, use the palm of your hand with an open hand (not a clenched fist) to avoid smashed fingers if the wrench slips.
  4. Never extend a wrench with a pipe or cheater bar unless the wrench is specifically rated for such use. Using a cheater bar on a standard wrench can cause the jaw to snap catastrophically.
  5. Keep cutting tools sharp. Dull tools require more force and are more likely to slip. Store sharp tools with blades covered or in a designated drawer.
  6. Do not carry tools in pockets. Use a tool belt or tool cart. Sharp or pointed tools in pockets can cause injury when bending or sitting.

24.5.4 Vehicle Support Safety (Jacks & Stands)

⚠ CRITICAL — LIFTING SAFETY: The single most deadly mistake in truck maintenance is working under a vehicle supported only by a hydraulic jack. A jack can fail suddenly due to seal blowout, hydraulic leak, or mechanical failure. ALWAYS use properly rated jack stands. Never use cinder blocks, concrete blocks, or stacked lumber as vehicle supports — they can crumble without warning.

Proper Lifting Procedure:

  1. Park on solid, level ground. Engage parking brake. Chock both sides of the wheels that remain on the ground.
  2. Identify correct lifting points on the frame rails or axle (refer to manufacturer's manual). Never lift on the differential, steering components, or body panels.
  3. Position the jack saddle with a block of wood (to prevent metal-on-metal contact and slipping).
  4. Lift the vehicle slowly and steadily. Watch for any shifting or instability.
  5. Once at desired height, place jack stands under the frame at the designated lift points. Use stands of the same type, in pairs, at the same height setting.
  6. Adjust the ratchet or pin mechanism — ensure it is fully engaged and cannot collapse.
  7. Gently lower the vehicle onto the stands. Do not lower with a bang. Lower until the full weight is on the stands and the jack is free.
  8. Test the stability by pushing the vehicle laterally at shoulder height. If it rocks, adjust stands or reposition.
  9. Only then may you work under the vehicle. The jack may be left in place as a secondary safety measure but must NOT bear the main load.

Jack Stand Ratings: Use stands rated for at least 1.5x the weight on that end of the vehicle. For a typical loaded tanker weighing 20,000 kg on the rear tandem: use stands rated at 30,000 lbs (13,600 kg) per pair minimum — two stands per axle.

  3. Never mix chemicals. Mixing bleach with ammonia produces deadly chlorine gas. Mixing acids with solvents can cause explosions. If in doubt, do not mix.
  4. Use proper dispensing equipment. Use pumps, funnels, or spouts — never pour from large containers without control. Use a bonding wire when transferring flammable liquids between metal containers (prevents static spark).
  5. Keep containers closed when not in use. Open containers evaporate, creating fire hazards and exposing workers to vapors.
  6. Waste disposal: Used oil, coolant, solvents, and oily rags are hazardous waste. Dispose of each in designated, labeled containers. Never pour chemicals down drains, onto the ground, or into trash bins.
  7. Spill response: Know the location of the spill kit. For chemical spills: contain with absorbent pads or booms. For fuel spills: eliminate all ignition sources. For acid spills: neutralize with baking soda before absorbing. Report spills to supervisor — documentation may be required for regulatory compliance.

24.7 Lockout / Tagout (LOTO) Procedure

Lockout/Tagout is used to ensure equipment is isolated from all energy sources before maintenance begins. The following energy sources exist in the workshop and on trucks:

  • Electrical: Battery power (12/24V), shore power, charger connections
  • Pneumatic: Compressed air in brake system, shop air lines
  • Hydraulic: Power steering, hydraulic lift gates, water brake systems
  • Mechanical (stored energy): Spring brake chambers, suspension springs, pressurized tanks
  • Thermal: Hot engine, exhaust, cooling system under pressure
  • Chemical: Fuel in tanks/lines, battery acid, refrigerants

Six-Step LOTO Procedure:

  1. NOTIFY all affected personnel that equipment will be locked out and why.
  2. SHUT DOWN the equipment using normal shutdown procedures (turn off engine, etc.).
  3. ISOLATE all energy sources: disconnect battery (negative first), close fuel valves, drain air pressure, release spring brakes, etc.
  4. LOCK & TAG each isolation point with your personal padlock and a tag stating: your name, date, reason, and expected return time. Tags must be weather-resistant and attached with a non-reusable zip tie or wire.
  5. VERIFY zero energy: attempt to start the engine (it should not start), test for voltage with a multimeter, verify air pressure is zero, manually release stored energy (e.g., pry against a spring with caution), check that the cooling system is depressurized.
  6. PROCEED with work. Only the person who applied the lock may remove it. To remove: complete the work, ensure all tools are cleared, ensure all guards are replaced, notify affected personnel, remove locks and tags.
⚠ Exception — "Hot Work" LOTO: Welding or grinding on a fuel tanker requires additional steps: purge the tank compartment (gas-free certificate), open all manlids for ventilation, continuously monitor with a combustible gas detector (LEL), have a fire watcher present with extinguisher, and secure a hot work permit from the supervisor.

24.8.2 PASS Extinguisher Operation

  1. Pull the pin — break the tamper seal.
  2. Aim at the base of the fire — not at the flames.
  3. Squeeze the handle — slowly and evenly.
  4. Sweep side to side — covering the entire base of the fire.

If the fire does not extinguish after one extinguisher, evacuate immediately and call emergency services (local fire department). Do not attempt to fight a fire that is spreading rapidly, involves gas cylinders, or is located in a confined space.

24.8.3 Fire Prevention in the Workshop

  • No smoking anywhere inside the workshop or within 10 meters of fuel storage areas.
  • Store flammable liquids in approved flammable storage cabinets. Cabinet doors must be self-closing and latched.
  • Oily rags: store in metal containers with self-closing lids. Empty daily.
  • Charging batteries: charge in a designated area with ventilation (hydrogen gas is explosive). Do not charge near flammables.
  • Electrical panels: keep clear — at least 1 meter of unobstructed space. Do not store anything against electrical panels.
  • No extension cords as permanent wiring. Use power bars with built-in circuit breakers.
  • Hot work (welding, grinding, cutting): clear combustibles, have extinguisher ready, post a fire watch for 30 minutes after work completes.
  • Fuel transfer: bond and ground containers. No smoking. Engine off. Have absorbent pads and a fire extinguisher positioned nearby.

24.9 Safe Lifting & Ergonomics

24.9.1 Manual Lifting — Proper Technique

  1. Size up the load: Before lifting, push or tilt the object to gauge its weight. If it is too heavy (over 23 kg / 50 lb for one person), get mechanical assistance (forklift, hoist, or second person).
  2. Stance: Feet shoulder-width apart, one foot slightly ahead of the other for stability.
  3. Bend your knees — not your back. Keep the natural curve of your spine (do not round your back).
  4. Grip firmly: Use full palm grip, not just fingertips. Wear gloves if the object has sharp edges.
  5. Lift with your legs: Straighten your legs while keeping your back straight. Keep the load close to your body.
  6. Do not twist: Turn by moving your feet, not by rotating your torso while carrying the load.
  7. Set down: Reverse the process — bend your knees, keep back straight, lower slowly.

25.2 Fire Emergency

  1. SHOUT "FIRE! FIRE! FIRE!" to alert everyone in the area.
  2. EVACUATE the immediate area. Close doors behind you if safe to do so (contains the fire).
  3. ACTIVATE the fire alarm if the workshop has one.
  4. ATTEMPT TO EXTINGUISH only if ALL of the following apply:
    • The fire is small (trash can size or smaller).
    • You have a clear escape route behind you.
    • You have been trained on extinguisher use (PASS method).
    • You are using the correct extinguisher for the fire class.
    • No gas cylinders, fuel tanks, or hazardous materials are involved.
  5. If fire grows, or if you cannot extinguish it: evacuate, call the fire department, and account for all personnel at the designated assembly point.
  6. DO NOT re-enter the building for any reason until fire officials declare it safe.

25.3 Fuel / Chemical Spill Emergency

  1. ASSESS the spill: is it a small drip (can be absorbed with a rag) or a major release (flowing liquid, large pool)?
  2. Small spill: absorb with rags or absorbent pads. Place absorbed material in a hazardous waste container. Clean area with degreaser.
  3. Major spill:
    • Protect drains: Place absorbent booms or drain covers around drains immediately.
    • Eliminate ignition sources: No smoking, no electrical equipment operation, turn off any running engines in the area.
    • Contain: Use absorbent booms to surround the spill and prevent spreading. Use granular absorbent to soak up liquid within the containment area.
    • Notify: Call the supervisor and safety officer immediately. For fuel spills over 25 liters, regulatory reporting may be required.
    • Clean up: Collect all contaminated absorbent materials into hazardous waste drums. Label and seal drums for proper disposal.
    • Document: Complete a spill report with: date, time, location, product spilled, estimated volume, cause, and corrective actions taken.

25.4 Injury / Medical Emergency

  1. Call for help: Shout for assistance or call emergency services. Do not move the injured person unless they are in immediate danger (fire, chemical exposure, collapsing structure).
  2. First Aid — life-threatening conditions (ABC):
    • A - Airway: Check if the person is conscious. Tilt head back, lift chin to open airway.
    • B - Breathing: Look, listen, and feel for breathing for 10 seconds. If not breathing normally, begin CPR (30 chest compressions at 100-120/min, then 2 rescue breaths).
    • C - Circulation: Control severe bleeding with direct pressure using a clean cloth or bandage. Raise the injured area above the heart if possible. Apply a tourniquet only for life-threatening limb bleeding that cannot be controlled with direct pressure.
  3. Chemical exposure:
    • Eyes: Flush with clean water from the eyewash station for at least 15 minutes. Hold eyelids open. Remove contact lenses if present.
    • Skin: Remove contaminated clothing. Rinse affected area with water for at least 15 minutes. Use soap and water for non-corrosive chemicals.
    • Inhalation: Move the person to fresh air immediately. If not breathing, begin CPR.
    • Ingestion: Call Poison Control. Do NOT induce vomiting unless instructed by Poison Control. Do not give anything by mouth to an unconscious person.
  4. Report: Every injury, no matter how minor, must be reported to the supervisor and recorded in the company's incident log. This is not optional — even a small cut can become infected, and reporting protects both the worker and the company.

26.1 Applicable Regulations

All petroleum transportation activities are subject to national and international dangerous goods regulations. The operator and driver are jointly responsible for compliance. Non-compliance can result in fines, vehicle impoundment, and denial of depot access.

Key regulations for petroleum tanker operations:

  • Dangerous Goods Regulations (DGR): National implementation of UN Model Regulations for transport of dangerous goods by road.
  • Depot Safety Rules: Each petroleum depot has its own safety requirements — these may vary and must be reviewed.
  • Environmental Regulations: Governing spill prevention, discharge reporting, and waste disposal.

  6. Driver HAZMAT Training Certificate: Proof of current dangerous goods awareness training.

26.5 Emergency Response Information

The driver must carry, and be familiar with, the Emergency Response Guide for the products being carried. At minimum:

  • ERG Guide 128 (Flammable Liquids — Non-Polar / Water-Immiscible) for petroleum products.
  • Emergency actions: For any spill or leak: stop engine, eliminate ignition sources, evacuate area, call emergency services, and provide the product information (UN number) and truck details.
  • Spill containment: Use on-board spill kit (absorbent pads, booms, drain covers). Do not wash product into drains or stormwater systems.
  • First aid: For inhalation (move to fresh air), skin contact (remove clothing, wash with soap and water), eye contact (flush with water 15 minutes), ingestion (do NOT induce vomiting — call poison control).

26.6 Customer / Receiving Site Safety

  1. Follow all site-specific safety rules (posted at the entrance).
  2. Verify the product and quantity before unloading.
  3. Connect grounding cable to the designated grounding point before opening any hatch or valve.
  4. Use only approved hoses and fittings for the product being unloaded.
  5. Chock wheels before disconnecting any hoses.
  6. Stay with the vehicle during the entire unloading process — never leave a truck unattended while it is connected for loading or unloading.
  7. Report any product loss, overfill, or leak immediately to the site supervisor and your dispatcher.

GVW / Gross Vehicle WeightThe total weight of the truck including chassis, body, payload, fuel, and occupants.
HAZMAT / Hazardous MaterialsDangerous goods that require special handling, placarding, and documentation during transport (e.g., petroleum products).

Interval ValueThe numeric value in the PM schedule that determines when a task becomes due, combined with the interval unit (km, hrs, days).
JO / Job OrderA digital work order in the system that records: truck, tasks, parts used, labor hours, and completion status.
KPI / Key Performance IndicatorA measurable value that tracks performance — in this system: jobs completed, hours worked, parts cost, etc.
LEL / Lower Explosive LimitThe minimum concentration of a flammable gas or vapor in air that can ignite. Used in confined space and tank entry monitoring.
LOTO / Lockout-TagoutA safety procedure that ensures equipment is isolated from all energy sources and locked to prevent accidental re-energization during maintenance.
LTO / Land Transportation OfficePhilippine government agency responsible for vehicle registration and driver licensing.
ManlidThe hinged cover on top of each tank compartment through which product is loaded (top-loaded trucks) or inspected.

QC / Quality ControlThe inspection process performed before returning a truck to service after any repair or PM.
SAE / Society of Automotive EngineersStandards organization that defines oil viscosity grades (e.g., SAE 15W-40) and fastener specifications.
SDS / Safety Data SheetA document that lists the properties, hazards, handling, and emergency procedures for a chemical product. Must be available for every chemical in the workshop.
Slack AdjusterA lever arm on the brake camshaft that converts the pushrod's linear motion into rotary motion to apply the brake shoes. Can be manual or automatic.
Spring BrakeA parking/emergency brake that uses a powerful spring to apply the brakes mechanically when air pressure is released (fall-safe design).
Torque SealA colored paint or lacquer applied to a tightened fastener to visually indicate that it has been torqued and has not moved.
U-Joint / Universal JointA flexible coupling that allows the driveshaft to transmit power at varying angles (necessary for suspension movement).

Vapor RecoveryA system that captures fuel vapors displaced during loading and returns them to the terminal, preventing vapor release to the atmosphere.
Water Brake (Retarder)A supplemental braking system that uses hydraulic resistance to slow the vehicle, preserving the service brakes for emergency stops. Common on heavy trucks in mountainous terrain.
